About me [also, blog post #1]

I am Sergey Golitsynskiy. This is my personal site. Right now most of it is about my coding projects, but I hope sooner rather than later it will become broader than that, with programming as its unifying theme.

I enjoy programming. I wrote some Basic in high school, and I got my Commodore 64 soon after school, but I only started writing actual programs in my late 20s, so I'm a late bloomer. I learned most of my CS foundations in Java, occasionally dipping into Scheme (thank you, Eugene; thank you, SICP) and a few other languages. Despite the Perils of JavaSchools, I'm very happy with the depth of my CS foundation that I got at UNI, besides, I did learn C later on.. I also spent years writing .NET web applications in C#, although occasionally I did some PHP as well. Nowadays I write mostly Python, JavaScript and, most recently, TypeScript. I don't have a favorite language; one thing I know I would hate is to end up using just one.

In terms of domain, a substantial part of my work has been (so far) on content management system architecture and, to some extent, generative programming, mostly in the context of web applications. I also wrote research code in a variety of areas - both as a student and, later, as an assistant professor - but only to discover, over and over again, that building the tools for research was by far more exciting than using them to do research to learn more about this or that phenomena. As Fred Brooks famously said, "scientists build to learn, engineers learn to build." Building things is what makes me happy and curious for more knowledge.

I do code for fun, and it's hard to come up with a definitive explanation why. CS for me was a conscious decision I've never regretted. I enjoy programming like I enjoyed exploring Tolkien's Middle-earth, cataloguing and rearranging my home library, or maybe even building toy cities as a child. To me, the fun comes from designing a structure, incrementally adding detail and simplifying the arrangement of the parts - and in the end having a complex, yet elegant "machine", that also "magically" comes to life and performs seemingly impossible tasks. Whether it's a report-generating app, or a system guiding the next Mars rover - it's the complexity of what lies beneath that makes it so interesting.

I live in beautiful Gaithersburg, MD, with my wife Alina, who is a postdoc at the University of Maryland (she is the real scientist); and Alice, our little daughter, who loves her books and stuffed animals, and learned the sl (steam locomotive) bash command at age 2 :-)
